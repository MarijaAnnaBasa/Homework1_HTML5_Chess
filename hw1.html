The History

Origins

Chess, as we know it today, was born out of the Indian game chaturanga before the 600s AD. The game spread throughout Asia and Europe over the coming centuries, and eventually evolved into what we know as chess around the 16th century.

An antique Chaturanga board and pieces

Creation and theory

Chess theory progressed slowly until the mid-18th century, when Francois-Andre Philidor published his book "Analyse du jeu des Échecs" in 1749. This book introduced new concepts for opening moves and a famous technique for endgames involving rooks and pawns, which is still used in chess today.

During the mid-1800s, the standardization of chess sets took place. Prior to the 1850s, chess sets varied greatly in their designs. In 1849, Jaques of London introduced a new style of chess pieces created by Nathaniel Cooke. These pieces, known as the Staunton pattern, were endorsed by Howard Staunton, the top player of that era. The Staunton pattern pieces became immensely popular and have since become the standard for chess sets used in tournaments around the world.

An early Jaques Staunton chess set

In the 19th century, chess clocks were introduced to competitive play. Prior to this development, a single chess game could last as long as 14 hours! With the adoption of chess clocks and the standardization of chess sets, the necessary equipment for modern matches and tournaments was established. World Chess Champions

Here is a list of the World Chess Champions from the middle of the 20th century to the present day:

  1. Mikhail Botvinnik (1948-1957, 1958-1960, 1961-1963);
  2. Vasily Smyslov (1957-1958);
  3. Mikhail Tal (1960-1961);
  4. Tigran Petrosian (1963-1969);
  5. Boris Spassky (1969-1972);
  6. Bobby Fischer (1972-1975);
  7. Anatoly Karpov (1975-1985);
  8. Garry Kasparov (1985-2000);
  9. Vladimir Kramnik (2000-2007);
  10. Viswanathan Anand (2007-2013);
  11. Magnus Carlsen (2013-present).

Chess Combinations

Chess combinations are powerful tactical sequences in chess that involve a series of moves to gain an advantage, often leading to winning material, checkmating the opponent, or securing a decisive position. They require precise calculation and foresight to execute successfully.

Some of the most famous chess combinations include:

  • 'Immortal Game' (Anderssen vs. Kieseritzky);
  • 'Evergreen Game' (Anderssen vs. Dufresne);
  • 'Opera Game' (Morphy vs. Duke of Brunswick & Count Isouard);
  • 'Sacrifice of the Century' (Alekhine vs. Vasic);
  • 'Game of the Century' (Fischer vs. Byrne);
  • 'Brilliancy Prize' (Kasparov vs. Topalov).

These combinations have become iconic in chess history, showcasing the beauty and creativity of tactical play on the board.
